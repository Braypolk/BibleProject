This week we finish Jeremiah!
Hey Reading Community,

We know, this book is long and dark, so we applaud you for sticking it out. Israel’s covenant unfaithfulness is what Jeremiah focuses on in the first half of the book, including their worship of other gods, widespread social injustice, and even some Israelites’ participation in the grotesque practice of child sacrifice. This culminates in chapter 25, with Jeremiah’s indictment against Israel and the surrounding nations. He announces the coming exile, where the Israelites will sit for 70 years. A message, if you finished reading last week, you know Israel’s leaders reject. Things are going downhill fast.

However, chapters 30-33 offer a very important intermission from the message of judgment with one of hope. The strategic placement of these encouraging words allude to the importance of this section; as you move through the rest of the book, which explores the actual siege and destruction of Israel, the message of hope should be echoing in the back of your mind. We encourage you to read this week’s blog on these key chapters in Jeremiah.


Onwards!

Jon, Tim, & The Bible Project Crew

Week 26
Summary

You concluded last week's reading with Israel’s rejection of Jeremiah and his message of judgment, and open this week with his message of hope. If you recall in Deuteronomy 30, Moses tells the Hebrew people that one day God will send them into exile, but they will eventually return, and he will circumcise their hearts. Jeremiah runs with this message and develops it, “I will place my Torah inside them, and write it on their hearts” (Jer 31:33). Not only will God bring his people home from the exile in which he must send them, he will also address the “heart” issue that has landed them there in the first place.

Chapters 46-51 are prophetic poems, which explore the judgment and destruction of the nations surrounding Jerusalem. Babylon is coming for the Philistines, the Egyptians, the Ammonites, and many others. However, chapters 50-51, the longest poems of judgment in this section, paradoxically focus on Babylon itself! While God is willing to use nations like Babylon to bring his justice to the world, he does not endorse their evil practices or idolatry. They are arrogant and rebellious, and they too will be brought down.
