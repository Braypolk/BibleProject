Hey Reading Community,

This week, you’ll finish two more Historical books from the Hebrew Bible, Nehemiah and Esther. Nehemiah, as you already know, recounts the return of the exiled Israelites from Babylon to Jerusalem. However, we think you'll find that despite Ezra and Nehemiah's attempts to reform the Israelite people, there was a heart condition that just did not get better, despite the exile. 

Esther explores God's providence working in the lives of the Jewish people still living in Babylon after the exile. The most curious thing about the book of Esther is that God is never mentioned. However, this is exactly what this week's blog explores. 

Onwards!

Week 30
Summary

Nehemiah is going to leave you with something to be desired. Despite the physical rebuilding of the walls, the rebuilding of the temple, and a number of reforms aimed at addressing the spiritual condition of the Israelite people who returned from exile. It is clear, there is a much larger heart condition that is still preventing the Israelites from truly loving and following Yahweh. 

Once you wrap up with the book of Nehemiah, you’ll be diving right into the book of Esther. The book of Esther is designed in perfect symmetry. Right down to every scene, you’ll find key-words and scenes that match the keywords in their partner scenes on the other side of the symmetry. It first builds up over the course of 7 dark scenes and then culminates with a pivot scene, followed by 7 more additional scenes with a different emotional tone.

Though God’s name is not mentioned, all of the events - the downfall of Vashti and rise of Esther, Mordecai’s discovery of the assassination plot, the short-lived exaltation of Haman, the insomnia of the king, the book read to the king reminding him of Mordecai’s heroics and the exaltation of the Jewish people - reflect the providential design of God’s handiwork moving history towards his redemptive end.
