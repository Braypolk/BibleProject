This week we finish Job and dive into Proverbs! 
Hey Reading Community,

You’re almost through the book of Job, a formidable literary piece. Ancient Hebrew poetry, especially on such fundamentally challenging topics as those covered in Job, are not easy to consume, nor digest. If you’re feeling a little lost, hang in there, because God is about to speak up again, and provide an answer to this ongoing debate between Job and his friends. It's important to remember the three questions on the line as you move into the final one-third of the book:
  1. God is Just and Good: God’s character compels him to always act justly for the good of others.
  2. The Retribution Principle: God has ordered the world, so that good deeds are rewarded, and evil deeds are punished.
  3. Job’s Innocence: Job has done nothing to deserve his suffering.
The problem is, not all of these can be true at the same time, so how will God answer? It might surprise you.

Onwards!

Week 22
Summary

Job and his “friends” have exhausted the best that Near Eastern philosophical thought has to offer, and it's time to land this plane. The book concludes with four final speeches: Job’s last speech (29-31), Elihu’s caution (32–37), Yahweh’s response to all these speeches (38-41), and a short reply from Job (42:1–6​).

Job: Job wants to believe God is just; however, since he still maintains his innocence, that doesn’t square with what he is experiencing if the divine retribution principle is in place. He simply demands an answer from God, who he believes may actually be... unjust?

Elihu: Without Elihu’s voice, readers might have a tendency to idealize Job and to conclude that his response to suffering was impeccable. While Job was innocent on the matter pertaining to his suffering, he did not respond as well as he could have. Don’t accuse God!

Yahweh: God gives an answer to Job, but not how you might expect, he never actually gets to why Job is suffering. In fact, God’s answer to Job does not even explain why righteous people might suffer at all because the cosmos is not designed to prevent righteous people from suffering. The purpose of the book of Job is not to provide an answer to why we suffer but gives us a model for how we can relate to God in the midst of suffering that we cannot explain.

While God does not endorse Job’s arrogant accusations, he does sympathetically describe Job’s struggle as “speaking rightly about me.” God condemns Job’s friends for presumptuously assuming they know how God works in the world. Job, finally, in his final speech, apologizes for questioning God and learns to trust God’s wisdom in all things.
