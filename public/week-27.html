This week we finish Lamentations and start Ezekiel!
Hey Reading Community,

You have officially crossed the halfway mark. Well done! Reading the Bible in one year takes serious dedication, and we applaud your effort. This week, there are two great, yet somber books that you will study. First, Lamentations: this is a unique book that contains five poetic reflections of the siege and destruction of Jerusalem and exile of the Jewish people by Babylon. It represents the collective voice of Israel crying out to God in protest, confusion, and the desire to understand why something this catastrophic has happened.

The second, much larger book you will open this week is Ezekiel. Ezekiel was in exile during the destruction of Jerusalem and so provides us with a different perspective than you get from any of the prophets living in Israel, either before or during the exile. What is most interesting about Ezekiel is the nature of God's call on Ezekiel to become a prophet, which is what this week's blog is on!

Onwards!

Jon, Tim, & The Bible Project Crew

Summary
There is no beating around the bush. Lamentations is a somber and emotional book. If you recall, the exile was the watershed moment for the Hebrew people and it would completely reshape the way they looked at the world, God, and their role in history. Lamentations both serves as a memorial to these cataclysmic events, but also provided the Israelites a means of processing their grief. This book consists of five poems using different characters and metaphors to explore these tragic events, but it doesn't provide any neat and tidy conclusions because the topics they explore are not neat and tidy.

The second book is Ezekiel. It is one of the most unique prophetic callings of any of the Old Testament prophets (ch. 1-2). This week, you will read chapters 1 through 15. Chapters 1-11 are a laundry list of accusations made against Israel in a variety of strange ways. These indictments against Israel are done through wild visions and extreme street theater.

In chapter 11, you will see that while God has to judge Israel for their unabashed sin, he is not abandoning them altogether. In fact, he will go with them into exile, thus offering a glimpse of hope. However, this won’t last long, as Ezekiel immediately launches into powerful judgement poetry through chapter 33. It will not be until chapter 34 that you see a dramatic shift in tone, so hold on to that hope!
