This week we finish Proverbs and dive into Ecclesiastes!
Hey Reading Community,

This week, you’ll become familiar with two new books from the Hebrew Wisdom Tradition, the books of Ecclesiastes and Song of Songs. These books are focused on two very different areas of our lives, but are intended to leave you with a newfound understanding about human experience and the world around us.

Ecclesiastes is going to introduce you to a critic who will try to turn your world upside down. You thought you’d get ahead by following the wisdom of the proverbs? Think again, because it doesn’t always work that way. While Ecclesiastes is an intensely wise book, it can leave you feeling a little low. Luckily, it’s followed up by the Song of Songs, a collection of ancient Israelite love poems that celebrate the beauty and power of God's gift of love and sexual desire.

Onwards!

Jon, Tim, & The Bible Project Crew

Week 24
Summary

Once you wrap up with the book of Proverbs on Day 1, you’ll be diving right into the book of Ecclesiastes. Ecclesiastes is primarily interested in exploring three different ideas, which initially seem to be at odds with the wisdom of Proverbs.

The primary character in Ecclesiastes, one could call the teacher or critic, believes that (1) time stops for no one. Rich or poor, young or old, famous or infamous, the world goes on and mostly forgets you ever existed. If you thought that was heavy, the critic also explores the concept of death. (2) Everyone dies, no exceptions. Man, what is the point then? Just wait, because the last point makes even less sense. (3) Life is random in nature. Proverbs sounds nice, but it doesn’t always work out that way. While all this might sound really depressing, the point is not. It is intended to help build humility, appreciation for the small things, and a healthy fear of the Lord.

This paves the way for the next book, the Song of Songs. This book is a beautiful exploration of love, sexual desire, and the longing and yearning of two lovers. It is written in the Wisdom Tradition of King Solomon. Some would say the metaphorical nature of this book might be pointing to God’s love for Israel, Jesus’ love of the church, but all we know for sure is that it does point to the divine gift of love within an idyllic Garden of Eden-type setting. Love is a powerful emotion and desire, and when the flame of love is contained within the proper boundaries of two who love one another fully, it is life-giving. However, if it gets out of hand, nothing can stop its destructive flames.
